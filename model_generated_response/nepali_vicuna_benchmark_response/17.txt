Instruction in English:  How do vaccines work to protect individuals and communities from infectious diseases, and what is herd immunity?.
                        Response in English: Vaccines work by stimulating the body's immune system to produce antibodies against a specific infectious agent, such as a virus or bacteria. When a person is vaccinated, their immune system recognizes the agent as a threat and produces antibodies to fight it. If the person is later exposed to the infectious agent, the antibodies will recognize and neutralize it, preventing the person from becoming sick.

Herd immunity refers to the protection of an entire community from an infectious disease when a high percentage of the population has been vaccinated. When a large portion of the population is immune to a disease, it becomes difficult for the disease to spread because there are fewer susceptible people to infect. Herd immunity helps to protect those who cannot be vaccinated, such as infants, the elderly, or those with weakened immune systems..
